Malé Island


Malé Island is the vibrant capital and largest city of the Republic of Maldives, a stunning nation of islands in the Indian Ocean. It's a highly unique destination, offering a stark contrast to the secluded luxury of the resort islands.

Key Facts

  • Densely Populated: Malé is one of the world's most densely populated cities. The entire island is virtually covered with colorful buildings, roads, and infrastructure, giving it a bustling, energetic atmosphere.
  • Small and Walkable: Despite the density, the central island is quite small, and you can easily walk around the entire perimeter in about an hour, making it perfect for exploration on foot.
  • Economic and Political Heart: It serves as the central hub for the Maldives, hosting the government and the main commercial port, with the economy largely driven by tourism and fishing.

Grand Friday Mosque


The Grand Friday Mosque in Malé, officially known as Masjid Al-Sultan Muhammad Thakurufaanu Al-Auzam, is the most prominent architectural and religious landmark in the Maldives.

Key Facts

  • Historical Context: It is a relatively modern structure, having been built and opened in 1984. It was financed and constructed with aid from several Gulf States and countries like Pakistan, Brunei, and Malaysia.
  • Central Landmark: It is the largest mosque in the Maldives and a central part of the Islamic Centre complex, located just a short walk from the main city harbor and Republic Square (Jumhooree Maidhaan).
  • Capacity: It is capable of accommodating up to 5,000 worshippers, serving as the main gathering place for Friday prayers.

Hulhumalé Island


Hulhumalé is a unique, artificially reclaimed island in the Maldives, strategically developed as a "City of Hope" to address the overcrowding of the capital, Malé, and to act as a modern, resilient urban center for the nation's future.

Key Facts

  • Reclaimed Land: Hulhumalé was created by pumping sand from the sea floor onto a submerged coral platform in the Hulhulé-Farukolhufushi lagoon, beginning in 1997.
  • Climate Resilience: It was designed with a much higher elevation (around 2 meters above sea level) compared to the average Maldivian island (most are 1 meter or less), making it a key part of the national strategy to adapt to rising sea levels.
  • Expansion of Malé: The island was developed as a planned city to relieve the population density of Malé, which is one of the world's most crowded capitals. It is planned to eventually house up to 240,000 inhabitants.

National Museum of the Maldives


The National Museum of the Maldives is the primary repository of the nation's cultural and historical heritage, located in the capital city of Malé, within the serene setting of Sultan Park.

Key Facts

  • Architecture: The museum complex features two main buildings: The Old Building, a three-story colonial-style structure that is the only remaining part of the 17th-century Maldivian Royal Palace compound. The New Building, a contemporary white structure that was designed and gifted by the Chinese government, and opened in 2010.
  • Royal Antiquities: A significant collection of royal regalia, including ceremonial thrones, palanquins (covered litter for transport), royal sunshades, and various items of furniture and clothing used by the former Sultans.
  • The museum offers a chronological journey through Maldivian history, with exhibits generally divided by era:Islamic Era: Features intricately carved wooden pieces with Arabic and Thaana inscriptions, commemorating the nation's conversion to Islam in 1153, as well as weaponry and religious paraphernalia.
