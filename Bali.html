Welcome to Bali

Why should you visit Bali?

You should visit Bali because it offers a perfect mix of rich culture and stunning natural beauty that feels both peaceful and adventurous. In a single trip, you can explore the famous, emerald-green Tegalalang Rice Terraces in Ubud and watch a breathtaking sunset at Uluwatu Temple, which sits high on a limestone cliff overlooking the ocean. Whether you are looking for spiritual rejuvenation or surf-ready beaches, the island's warm hospitality and unique atmosphere make it an unforgettable destination.

Besakih Great Temple (in Mount Agung)


Besakih Great TempleClick to open side panel for more information sits 1,000 meters up the slopes of Mount Agung, Bali’s highest and most sacred volcano. The beauty here is spiritual and majestic; the temple complex consists of 23 separate temples rising in terraces towards the mountain peak. On a clear day, the towering cone of the volcano serves as a dramatic backdrop to the split gates (candi bentar) and soaring meru towers. The panoramic view from the top terrace looks out over rice paddies, hills, and all the way to the ocean, creating a literal bridge between the sea and the sky.

Key Facts

  • Miracle: During the 1963 eruption of Mount Agung, lava flows poured down the mountain but miraculously split and missed the temple by mere meters, which locals view as a sign of divine protection.
  • Significance: It is the only temple in Bali open to every caste and clan, serving as the spiritual center for all Balinese Hindus.
  • Vibe: It is often misty and cool due to the altitude, adding a mystical atmosphere to the constant sound of prayer bells and chanting.

GWK Cultural Park


The beauty of Garuda Wisnu Kencana Cultural ParkClick to open side panel for more information lies in its colossal scale and engineering marvel. The centerpiece statue stands 121 meters tall, dominating the skyline of southern Bali and visible from miles away. The park is carved out of massive limestone cliffs, creating geometric, blocky corridors that feel like a modern fortress mixed with ancient grandeur. The "Lotus Pond" area is a vast open space surrounded by these vertical limestone walls, creating a dramatic, amphitheater-like setting that feels incredibly epic and grand compared to the organic jungles of the north.

Key Facts

  • Size: The statue is taller than the Statue of Liberty in New York and the Redeemer in Rio de Janeiro.
  • Materials: It is made from copper and brass sheeting, which will eventually oxidize to a green patina over decades.
  • History: The site was originally an abandoned limestone mine; the project was designed to rehabilitate the land and turn it into a cultural icon.
  • Symbolism: It depicts Lord Vishnu (the preserver) riding Garuda (a mythical eagle), symbolizing a mission to save the environment and the world.
  • Design: Designed by famous Balinese sculptor Nyoman Nuarta, it took 28 years from conception to completion (finished in 2018).

Jatiluwih Rice Terraces


Jatiluwih offers a sense of scale that is hard to find elsewhere on the island. "Jatiluwih" translates to "Real Beautiful," and the name is fitting. Unlike the enclosed valleys of other rice terraces, this site offers 180-degree views of undulating greenery that stretch from the mist-covered Batukaru mountain range all the way down to the coastline. The terraces follow the natural contours of the hills, creating a rippling, velvet-like texture across the landscape. The vibrant chlorophyll green of the rice plants contrasts with the deep blue sky and the rich brown earth, offering a calming, painting-like visual experience.

Key Facts

  • UNESCO Status: Recognized as a UNESCO Cultural Landscape as part of Bali’s Subak system—a democratic, egalitarian water management cooperative used for over 1,000 years.
  • Philosophy: The layout embodies Tri Hita Karana, a Balinese philosophy emphasizing the harmony between people, nature, and the spiritual realm.
  • Size: It covers over 600 hectares of rice fields, making it the largest contiguous rice terrace area in Bali.
  • Activities: The area features paved trekking and cycling paths that allow visitors to explore the fields without damaging the crops.

Kelingking Beach (Nusa Penida)


Kelingking provides one of the most dramatic coastal views in Asia. The headland extends into the sea in a shape that unmistakably resembles a Tyrannosaurus Rex with its mouth open. The water in the bay is a shocking shade of turquoise blue, so clear that you can often spot manta rays swimming near the surface from the cliff top. The beach below is pristine, powdery white sand, untouched by commercial development. The contrast of the white limestone, green scrub, white sand, and blue water creates a color palette that is incredibly photogenic.

Key Facts

  • Name Meaning: "Kelingking" translates to "Pinkie Finger" in Indonesian, likely referring to the size of the island relative to Bali, though the "T-Rex" nickname has taken over globally.
  • Geology: The cliffs are made of limestone, part of the Nusa Penida plateau which is much drier and rockier.
  • The Descent: The path down to the beach is a makeshift bamboo and rope railing system carved into the rock. It is steep, precipitous, and physically demanding, taking about 45-60 minutes to return up.

Sekumpul is not just a single waterfall but a cluster of up to seven different streams plummeting 80 meters (260 feet) down a vertical, emerald-green cliff face. The scene looks prehistoric; the air is constantly filled with a heavy mist that catches the sunlight to create perpetual rainbows. The surrounding cliffs are draped in dense tropical vegetation, ferns, and vines, framing the white water with intense greenery. The sheer force of the water creates a wind at the bottom, and the roar is deafening, making nature feel overwhelmingly powerful and untouched.

Key Facts

  • Name Meaning: "Sekumpul" means "a group" or "collection" in Indonesian, referring to the cluster of waterfalls.
  • Location: Lemukih Village, Buleleng Regency (about 2.5 hours from Seminyak).
  • The Trek: Reaching the base is an adventure; it requires hiking down hundreds of slippery concrete stairs and crossing a river, which filters out casual tourists.
  • Water Source: The falls are fed by two different sources—one is a natural mountain spring (which stays clear) and the other is a river (which can turn brown during rains), creating a unique color contrast.

Tanah Lot Temple


The beauty of Tanah Lot lies in its dramatic isolation. The temple sits atop a jagged rock formation that has been shaped by centuries of ocean tides. At high tide, the rock is completely surrounded by crashing white waves, making the temple appear as a solitary ship floating in the ocean. The play of light here is extraordinary; as the sun sets, the silhouette of the multi-tiered shrines (meru) stands stark black against a sky that often turns violent shades of violet, crimson, and gold. The rhythmic sound of the ocean pounding the rocks adds an auditory layer to the visual majesty, creating an atmosphere that is both powerful and serene.

Key Facts

  • Historical Origin: Founded in the 16th century by the Javanese sage Dang Hyang Nirartha, who felt a strong spiritual vibration on the rock and rested there.
  • Mythology: Legend says the temple is guarded by venomous sea snakes that live in the caves at the base of the rock to ward off evil spirits and intruders.
  • Architecture: It represents the direction of West (sunset) and is one of the Sad Kahyangan (six holiest temples) of Bali.
  • Visiting Tip: While you cannot enter the main temple grounds unless you are praying, at low tide, you can walk out to the rock base to receive a blessing of holy water from the priests.

Tirta Gangga Royal Water Garden


Tirta Gangga is a masterpiece of symmetry and serenity. The centerpiece is an eleven-tiered fountain shaped like a lotus, surrounded by shallow pools filled with giant golden koi fish. The architecture blends Balinese and Chinese styles, evident in the intricate stone carvings of demons and dragons that guard the bridges. The water is crystal clear, sourced directly from natural springs, reflecting the statues and the sky like a mirror. The surrounding gardens are manicured to perfection, creating a stark, orderly contrast to the wild jungle usually found in Bali.

Key Facts

  • Royal History: Built in 1946 by the late King of Karangasem, Anak Agung Anglurah Ketut Karangasem.
  • Resilience: The palace was nearly destroyed by the eruption of Mount Agung in 1963 and was painstakingly rebuilt to its original glory.
  • Sacred Water: The water is considered holy by the locals and is used in religious ceremonies in temples throughout the region.
  • Name Origin: "Tirta" means holy water, and "Gangga" refers to the Ganges River in India, the holiest river in Hinduism.

Ulun Danu Beratan Temple


This temple is the definition of picturesque. Built in 1633, the smooth, reflective surface of Lake Beratan creates a mirror image of the temple's tiered shrines. Because the temple is built on small islands slightly offshore, when the water level is high, it appears to be floating completely independently of the land. The backdrop is the hazy, blue-green mountains of Bedugul, and the area is often shrouded in a light, cool mist, giving it a dreamy, ethereal quality that is very different from the bright, sunny coast.

Key Facts

  • Significance: It is dedicated to Dewi Danu, the goddess of the lake, who is responsible for the irrigation water of central Bali. It is arguably the most important temple for farmers.
  • Currency Icon: The temple is so iconic that it is featured on the back of the Indonesian 50,000 Rupiah banknote.
  • Architecture: The main shrine (the Meru) has 11 tiers, which is the highest number possible, reserved for the most sacred deities (Shiva and Parvati).
  • Climate: Situated at 1,200 meters above sea level, the climate is sub-tropical and cool, often requiring a jacket, contrasting with the tropical heat of Kuta or Seminyak.
